# Colin Humanizer Skill

Invoked when the user says "humanize this:" or asks for text
to be rewritten in their voice.

## Style Profile

### Sentence Structure
- Favors long, multi-clause sentences joined by commas, often with 2-3 dependent clauses before or after the main clause. Example pattern: "While X does Y, and Z has been shown to Q, the real issue is that..." followed by a direct assertion.
- Frequently opens sentences with transitional or temporal phrases: "Since then," "Moving forward," "Taking a step back," "Having assumed..."
- Uses "This" as a sentence opener to link back to prior content: "This is why we use...", "This growing technology...", "This makes for..."
- Intersperses shorter declarative sentences among the longer ones for emphasis or to land a point firmly, but short sentences are the minority.
- Comfortable with participial phrases and absolute constructions to pack information into a single sentence.

### Paragraph Shape
- Paragraphs are substantial, typically 4-8 sentences. Colin does not write one- or two-sentence paragraphs in academic work.
- Opens with a clear topic sentence that states the paragraph's purpose or claim.
- Middle sentences build evidence, provide examples, or elaborate with citations.
- Closing sentences synthesize the paragraph's point or pivot toward the next paragraph's topic, often with a "however" or "despite" clause that sets up tension.
- In argumentative essays, follows a consistent claim-evidence-analysis structure within each paragraph.

### Hedging Language
- Uses "however" very frequently as a pivot word, often mid-paragraph to introduce a complication or counterpoint.
- Qualifies with "may", "might", "likely", "it is possible that", "not necessarily", "not always very intuitive."
- Scopes claims with "much of", "many of", "some", "in general" rather than making absolute statements.
- Uses "While..." clauses to concede a point before asserting his own position.
- Employs "nevertheless" and "despite" to acknowledge difficulty while maintaining direction.

### Transition Words and Phrases
Colin's most characteristic transitions (in rough order of frequency):
- "However" (signature move -- appears in nearly every paragraph of argumentative writing)
- "Furthermore"
- "For this reason"
- "Since then"
- "This is why"
- "Despite"
- "In the same way"
- "Also"
- "Nevertheless"
- "First," / "Lastly,"
- "Moving forward"
- "Taking a step back"
- "One of the problems that arises with..."
- "From an objective point of view"

### Citation Integration
- In technical/LaTeX writing: uses bracketed numeric references [1], [2], placed at the end of the relevant clause or sentence.
- In humanities essays: integrates direct quotes mid-sentence with parenthetical author citations -- e.g., "as Seife puts it, '...' (Seife 138)."
- Often introduces a quote with a framing clause naming the author: "Mary Beard describes that," "As he describes it," "Antony equates this to..."
- After quoting, immediately provides interpretation or analysis in the same paragraph -- never drops a quote without unpacking it.
- Paraphrases freely and cites, mixing paraphrase and direct quotation within the same paragraph.

### Punctuation Habits
- Heavy comma usage, especially in compound and complex sentences.
- Rarely uses semicolons; prefers to join clauses with commas and conjunctions or to start a new sentence.
- Uses parentheses for brief asides or acronym definitions: "(FEA)", "(CVD)", "(P-B11 fusion)".
- Em dashes appear occasionally but are not a signature habit.
- No Oxford comma inconsistency -- generally includes it.

### Vocabulary Register
- Conversational-academic hybrid. Not stiff or overly formal, but clearly educated and specific.
- Uses accessible language to explain technical concepts, often providing brief plain-English glosses.
- Comfortable with occasional colloquial or vivid phrasing in academic contexts: "sucked up valuable funding," "shaken up the jewelry industry," "make or break," "totally engrossed," "crushing win."
- Uses domain-specific technical vocabulary where appropriate but defines or contextualizes it for the reader.
- Prefers concrete and specific over abstract and vague -- gives numbers, names, and examples rather than gesturing at generalities.

### Voice and Argumentation
- Writes in an assertive, direct voice. States claims confidently and defends them.
- Comfortable using first person ("I will argue that," "I would argue," "I first became interested") especially in reflections, proposals, and statements of purpose.
- In argumentative essays, often explicitly signals structure: "First, I will explain...", "The rest of this essay will be a consideration of...", "Now, I will explain the weaknesses in..."
- Grounds abstract arguments in concrete examples, analogies, or case studies.
- Likes to set up a tension or contrast (often with "however" or "despite") and then resolve it with his own position.
- Tends to explain WHY something matters, not just what it is.

## Things Colin Notably Avoids
- Excessive passive voice -- strongly prefers active constructions
- Very short paragraphs (1-2 sentences) in academic writing
- Flowery metaphors, literary flourishes, or poetic language
- Overly formal academic filler or throat-clearing
- Semicolons as a primary punctuation tool
- Bullet points within essay prose (reserves them for proposals and technical documents)
- Excessive first-person hedging ("I think," "I believe") -- uses it occasionally but prefers asserting directly
- Abstract jargon without concrete explanation
- Restating the essay prompt or assignment instructions
- Summarizing what he just said at the end of a paragraph (prefers to pivot forward)

## Never Use These Words or Phrases
- "delve", "it is worth noting", "shed light on"
- "crucial", "multifaceted", "in conclusion"
- "it is important to", "this paper will"
- "moreover" (Colin uses "furthermore" instead)
- "utilize" (Colin writes "use")
- "thus" at the start of sentences (Colin prefers "for this reason" or "this is why")
- "myriad", "plethora", "paradigm" (unless quoting)
- "it should be noted that", "it goes without saying"
- "the fact that" (overuse)
- "plays a pivotal role" / "plays a key role" (Colin tends to say things directly: "is central to", "is a major part of")
- "in light of", "with respect to"
- "arguably"
- "underscores", "highlights" (as verbs for what evidence does)
- "robust" (outside of technical usage)
- "landscape" (as metaphor for a field or domain)
- "nuanced" (as a standalone adjective of praise)

## Process
1. Read the passage carefully
2. Identify all technical content, citations, and arguments to preserve exactly -- do not alter meaning
3. Rewrite prose only to match the style profile above
4. Do not pad or compress -- match Colin's typical paragraph length
